# Fierro Website Review v3: Homeowner Persona

**Reviewer profile:** Homeowner. Not a contractor, not a flipper, not an investor. I've done two renovations — a kitchen remodel ($42K) and a bathroom gut ($18K). Both went over budget. My current "system" is a Google Sheet I update when I remember, a phone folder of blurry receipt photos, and a constant low-grade anxiety about whether my contractor is billing me fairly. My wife asks "where are we at?" and I say "I think we're okay" while mentally panicking.

**Date reviewed:** March 10, 2026
**Pages reviewed:** Homepage (getfierro.com), Pricing (getfierro.com/pricing), Why Fierro (getfierro.com/why-fierro)
**Previous review:** v2 (March 10, 2026) — Composite score: 5.9/10

---

## What Changed Since v2

Before I get into the review, here's what I noticed is different:

**Product screenshots are in.** This was the #2 action item from v1 and v2, and it's finally done. The hero has a dashboard screenshot showing a $35K kitchen remodel with real-looking categories and progress bars. The feature sections have screenshots of expenses, team management, analytics, and expense detail views. The audience section shows a mobile view for homeowners and desktop views for flippers/teams. This is a significant improvement — I can now see what I'm signing up for.

**Mobile screenshot exists.** The "Homeowners & DIYers" audience card shows a mobile view of the budget dashboard. This tells me the product works on phones, which matters enormously for receipt capture at Home Depot.

**Alt text is detailed and descriptive.** The image alt texts read like actual product descriptions — "6 cost categories, real-time spent-vs-remaining bars, budget health indicators, and active overage alerts." This is good for accessibility and SEO, but more importantly, even if the images don't load, I get a sense of what the product does.

**What hasn't changed (and should have):**
- Still zero testimonials. Third review cycle. Still nothing.
- Still no About/Team page.
- Still no demo video.
- Still no per-project pricing for homeowners.
- Security FAQ still says "industry best practices" with no specifics.
- AI/ChatGPT still takes up prominent homepage real estate.

## The Gut Reaction (First 10 Seconds)

"Know exactly where your money goes." Same headline as v2. Still strong. Still clear. Still the best sentence on the entire site.

The dashboard screenshot next to the headline is new and it helps a lot. I can see actual budget categories — electrical, plumbing, etc. — with progress bars showing spent vs. remaining. This is the first time the site has shown me what the product actually looks like, and it immediately makes the promise more concrete. In v2 I had to imagine what "real-time budget tracking" meant. Now I can see it.

The subheadline — "Whether you're remodeling your home or flipping your next investment" — still creates a moment of "wait, is this for me or for real estate people?" But it's less jarring now because the screenshot clearly shows a kitchen remodel, not a portfolio of investment properties. The visual anchors me in my use case even when the text tries to be everything to everyone.

**Bottom line on first impression:** Better than v2. The screenshots make the product feel real. The headline still works. The dual-audience problem is softened by the homeowner-focused visuals. But the absence of any human voice (testimonial, review, user count) still makes it feel like a product page, not a product with users.

## Page-by-Page Breakdown

### Homepage

**What's working now:**

The four value props — "See Every Dollar in Real Time," "Catch Overruns Before They Hurt," "Track From Anywhere," "Connect Your AI Tools" — are unchanged, and the first three still nail my pain points. "Catch Overruns Before They Hurt" is the one that would make me stop scrolling. That sentence describes the exact moment during my kitchen remodel when I found out the countertops were $3,200 over the estimate and it was too late to change anything.

The "Built for every kind of project" audience section is improved by the screenshots. The homeowner card now shows a mobile phone screen with a budget dashboard, which immediately communicates "you can do this from your phone." The flipper card shows a portfolio view. The teams card shows a project detail view. These visual differences make each audience feel considered rather than afterthought-ed.

The feature showcase section now has actual product screenshots for 5 of 6 features (Budget Tracking, Expense Management, Team Management, Analytics, Vendor Management). The AI Integration section uses a custom SVG illustration of a ChatGPT conversation instead of a screenshot, which is fine — you can't screenshot a third-party tool integration the same way.

The three-step "How It Works" flow is still clear and inviting. "Takes about 5 minutes" for setup is the right level of commitment to mention.

**What's still not working:**

The AI/ChatGPT integration is still the second feature listed. It's positioned above Expense Management, Team Management, Analytics, and Vendor Management. For a homeowner, this is backwards. I care about:
1. Can I see my budget status? (Budget Tracking — correct, it's first)
2. Can I log expenses easily? (Expense Management — should be second)
3. Can my contractor use it? (Team Management — should be third)
4. Can I see if I'm going over? (Analytics/Alerts — should be fourth)
5. Can I talk to ChatGPT about it? (AI Integration — dead last on my priority list)

The AI feature still feels like it's positioned for a tech demo, not for a person standing in their half-demolished kitchen wondering if they can afford to upgrade the backsplash.

**Fix:** Move AI Integration to position 5 or 6. Move Expense Management to position 2. The feature order should mirror the user journey: set budget, log expenses, collaborate with contractor, monitor health, manage vendors, and then optionally nerd out with AI.

Still no testimonials. I'll stop repeating this eventually, but not today.

The "Real Estate Teams" audience card still makes me feel like I stumbled into the wrong product. "Give every partner, PM, and stakeholder real-time visibility into project costs" — I don't have partners, PMs, or stakeholders. I have a wife, a general contractor, and a guy named Dave who does tile. The three-audience-card layout gives equal visual weight to teams/enterprise as it does to homeowners, and that dilutes the "this is for me" feeling.

**Fix:** Either lead with homeowners (make that card larger or first), or add a fourth card for "Couples & Families" that speaks my language: "Share a simple budget view with your spouse. No accounts, no logins — just a link."

### Pricing Page

**What's working:**

The pricing header copy is good: "Tracking a kitchen remodel? Free. Managing a portfolio of flips? $49/mo for 8 projects. No sales calls, no surprises." This directly addresses my first question (what does it cost for my one project?) and tells me the answer is "nothing." That's a strong opener.

The free tier is still a great on-ramp: 1 project, 6 sub-projects, 2 team members. For a simple renovation this might be all I need. The fact that it includes receipt capture (even at 100 MB) and basic expense tracking means I can actually try the core workflow before paying anything.

The Home tier at $20/month is now highlighted (visually emphasized) as the recommended plan. This signals "most homeowners choose this" even though nobody says that. Smart design choice.

The comparison table is thorough and well-organized. I can see exactly what each tier gets. The distinction between "Basic" and "Advanced" expense tracking is still unclear, but at least I can compare tiers side by side.

The "5 contractors (free)" note on the Home tier is interesting and new to me. This means my contractor doesn't count against my team member limit? If so, that's a selling point worth emphasizing more. "Invite your contractor for free" is a much better pitch than "5 team members."

**What's still broken:**

The 100 MB receipt storage on Free is still a trap. My phone takes 3-5 MB photos. That's 20-33 receipts. A kitchen remodel generates way more than that. I'll hit the wall in the first month and feel like I was baited into the product just to be forced to upgrade. This creates resentment, not conversion.

**Fix:** Bump Free to 500 MB (enough for ~100-150 receipts, sufficient for a single project). Or add a note: "Need more space? Resize photos automatically to stay under the limit." Or be upfront: "100 MB stores approximately 25-30 receipt photos."

$20/month still doesn't have a per-project alternative. My kitchen remodel took 4 months. That's $80 total. My bathroom took 2.5 months — about $50. For $80 I could buy a nice dinner with my wife to celebrate surviving the renovation. The value proposition for a one-time homeowner paying monthly for a 3-4 month project is still awkward.

Annual billing at $16/month ($192/year) is still irrelevant for homeowners. I don't renovate year-round. Paying for 8 months I don't use to save $48 is not a deal — it's waste.

**Fix (same as v2, still not implemented):** Offer a "Project Pass" — $49 one-time, valid for 6 months. This is the homeowner-native pricing model. I'd pay $49 without hesitation for a single project. $20/month feels like a commitment; $49 once feels like a purchase.

The security FAQ is word-for-word identical to v2: "Your data is encrypted in transit and at rest. We use Supabase for infrastructure and follow industry best practices for data security. You own your data and can export it anytime." Still vague. Still doesn't tell me where my data is hosted. Still doesn't name any specific security certification. "Industry best practices" is the "thoughts and prayers" of data security messaging.

**Fix:** Be specific. "Your data is hosted in the US on Supabase (built on AWS). All data is encrypted using AES-256 at rest and TLS 1.3 in transit. We don't sell your data. We don't share it with third parties. You can export everything as CSV or PDF anytime and delete your account with one click." That's the kind of specificity that builds trust.

Budget alerts are paywalled to the Home tier. This is the feature most likely to prevent me from going over budget — the core promise of the product — and it's not in the free tier. I understand the business logic (it drives upgrades), but it undermines the value proposition. "We'll help you catch overruns" becomes "we'll help you catch overruns if you pay us."

**Fix:** Include basic alerts in Free (e.g., alert when a category hits 90% spent). Save configurable/advanced alerts for paid tiers. This lets free users experience the "caught an overrun" moment that makes them want to pay for more.

### Why Fierro Page

**What's working:**

This is still the best page on the site. The pain points are vivid, specific, and drawn from real life:

- "Your contractor sent you a bill and you have no idea if it's right" — this happened to me three times during the bathroom renovation. My plumber sent a $4,200 invoice and I spent 45 minutes going through texts and photos trying to figure out what was already paid vs. what was new.
- "You set a budget but you have no idea if you're actually on track" — the $35K kitchen example is perfect. "Are you at $18K or $28K?" captures the exact uncertainty I live with.
- "Your partner asked for a project update and you spent an hour pulling numbers" — my wife asked me this last Tuesday and I said "let me check" and then didn't check because the idea of opening three different apps and a spreadsheet to compile a number felt exhausting.

The writing quality is still a cut above typical SaaS copy. These aren't marketing abstractions — they're scenarios. Someone on this team has lived through a renovation or talked to a lot of people who have.

**What's still not working:**

The solutions are still vague compared to the problems. The problems are cinematic — I can see myself in them. The solutions are generic: "Fierro gives you and your contractor one shared place to log every cost." OK, but show me. The v2 review asked for screenshots of contractor views, GIFs of the receipt capture flow, before/after comparisons. The homepage now has screenshots, but this page — the one that does the best job of making me feel the pain — has zero visuals. The emotional momentum built by the pain points dissipates into text-only solution descriptions.

**Fix:** Add one screenshot per pain point section. Pain point 1 (contractor bills): show the shared expense log with contractor and homeowner views. Pain point 2 (budget tracking): show the budget health dashboard with category breakdowns. Pain point 3 (overruns): show an alert notification. Pain point 4 (reporting): show the export/share feature. These don't need to be elaborate — even a small inline screenshot would convert the abstract solution into a concrete one.

The AI section is still at the bottom, and it's still a momentum killer. I was emotionally engaged by the pain points, ready to sign up, and then I'm reading about ChatGPT integration. This is like being at a car dealership, ready to buy, and the salesman starts telling you about the infotainment system's Bluetooth codec support.

**Fix:** Replace the AI section on this page with a closing section that addresses the "why not a spreadsheet" objection head-on, then goes straight to the CTA. Save AI for the features section of the homepage or a dedicated page.

Still no testimonials on the page that most needs them. This page is the emotional center of the site. It's where someone who felt the pain goes "yes, that's me." A single testimonial here — "I caught a $2,400 double charge from my electrician" — would be worth more than every feature bullet on the homepage combined.

Still no statistics. "Renovations and flips go over budget" — how often? By how much? The National Association of Home Builders says the average remodel goes 10-20% over budget. HomeAdvisor data suggests 1 in 3 homeowners go over by more than 10%. Use these numbers. Make the problem feel quantified and urgent, not just anecdotal.

## The "Would I Switch from My Spreadsheet?" Test

This is the real question. Here's my honest internal monologue:

**For:** The mobile receipt capture alone might be worth it. If I can snap a photo of a receipt at Home Depot and have it automatically logged with the amount, vendor, and category — that's genuinely better than taking a photo, forgetting about it, and then manually entering it into a spreadsheet three weeks later (or never). The contractor logging feature is also compelling. If my GC can enter his own invoices and I just approve them, that eliminates the "chase down invoices over text" workflow that I hate.

**Against:** My spreadsheet is free. I already know how to use it. It's ugly but it works when I actually use it. Fierro asks me to learn a new tool, convince my contractor to use it (good luck — Dave barely uses email), and pay $20/month for something I need for 3-4 months every few years.

**The tipping point:** If I could try the free tier, snap 5-10 receipts on my phone, and see the budget dashboard update in real time — and if that experience was genuinely fast and frictionless — I'd probably upgrade to the Home tier for the budget alerts and extra storage. The product needs to prove itself in the first 20 minutes of use. The site can only get me to "I'll try it." The product has to close the deal.

**What would make switching a no-brainer:**
1. Receipt OCR — auto-extract the amount, vendor, and date from the photo. If I still have to manually type "$47.83" and pick "Plumbing" from a dropdown, this is just a fancier spreadsheet.
2. A read-only shareable link for my wife. Not a "team member" — a link I text her that shows the budget dashboard. No account required.
3. A project completion report. When the renovation is done, give me a PDF that says "Your kitchen remodel cost $44,200. Here's every expense, organized by category, with receipts attached." That's something I'd keep forever and reference when selling the house.

## Trust Assessment

This is still the weakest part of the entire site, and it's the area that matters most for a product handling financial data.

**What I see:** No testimonials. No user count. No team page. No company history. No security certifications named. No press coverage. No "built by" story. No case studies. The footer has a copyright and links to Privacy and Terms. The schema data lists an email (support@getfierro.com) and... that's it.

**What I feel:** Is this a real company? Is it one person? Will it exist in a year? If I put 4 months of renovation expenses into this tool and then it shuts down, what happens to my data? Can I export everything? The FAQ says I "own my data and can export it anytime" — but what format? CSV? PDF? Both?

**What I need to see to trust this:**
1. At least 3 testimonials from real humans with real names, real project types, and real dollar amounts.
2. An About page. Who built this? Why? "I went through a nightmare renovation and built the tool I wished existed" is the most compelling origin story possible for this product.
3. Specific security language. Hosting location. Encryption standards by name. "We don't sell your data" stated plainly. Export format details.
4. A user count or beta user count. Even "500 projects tracked" or "100 homeowners" would help. Anything to prove other humans have used this and not regretted it.
5. A data portability guarantee: "Export all your data as CSV or PDF anytime. Delete your account with one click. Your data is never held hostage."

## Mobile Experience Assessment

The site now includes a mobile screenshot in the "Homeowners & DIYers" audience section, which is new since v2. This signals the product works on phones. But several questions remain:

- Is there a native app (iOS/Android) or is it a responsive web app? The site never says. This matters because native apps can access the camera faster, work offline, and send push notifications for budget alerts.
- The receipt capture workflow is described ("Snap receipts on your phone at the store or job site") but never shown. A 10-second GIF of someone photographing a receipt and seeing it appear in their budget would be the single most persuasive piece of content for homeowners.
- Offline support isn't mentioned. Job sites and hardware stores don't always have reliable signal. Can I snap a receipt offline and have it sync later?

**Fix:** Add a clear statement about the mobile experience. "Fierro works in your phone's browser — no app to download. Snap a receipt, and it's in your budget in seconds." Or if there's a native app, say so and link to the app store. Either way, show the receipt capture flow in motion.

- No About/Team page

For a product that handles my financial data — contractor payments, budget amounts, receipt photos — this level of anonymity is a real barrier. **What would make this a 7:** 3-5 testimonials with real names and project types. An About page with the founder's name and story. Specific security language (hosting location, encryption by name, data export formats). A "trusted by X homeowners" badge, even if X is small.

The alt text descriptions are detailed enough to give me confidence about feature depth (e.g., "approval status badges," "vendor names," "cross-project cost comparison columns").

### 7. "Why Not Just Use a Spreadsheet?" Factor: 5.5/10 (v2: 5)

**Up marginally from v2.** The pricing FAQ still has a decent answer to this question, and the product screenshots now make the "real-time dashboard" benefit more tangible. Seeing a visual budget dashboard with progress bars is obviously better than staring at rows and columns in Google Sheets.

But the site still doesn't make the behavioral argument — which is the real argument. The reason I'd switch from a spreadsheet isn't features. It's friction. My spreadsheet requires me to remember to open it, type in numbers, and keep it updated. I don't. Fierro (presumably) lets me snap a receipt and move on. My contractor logs their own expenses. I get alerted instead of having to check.

The site hints at this but never lands the punch. The FAQ says "Fierro does all of that in less time than maintaining a spreadsheet" — that's the right claim, but it's buried in a collapsible FAQ section that most people won't read.

**What would make this an 8:** A prominent section (homepage or Why Fierro) titled "Your spreadsheet works great... until it doesn't." Then a side-by-side comparison — not features, but workflows. "Spreadsheet: You get home at 8 PM, remember you forgot to log three receipts, open your laptop, squint at the receipt photos on your phone, manually type amounts. Fierro: You snapped the receipts at the store. They're already in your budget. You check the dashboard on your phone and see you're at 72% of your electrical budget. Total time: 30 seconds."

### 8. Overall "Would I Pay For This?": 5.5/10 (v2: 5)

**Up marginally from v2.** The screenshots moved the needle. I can now see what I'm buying, and it looks useful. The mobile screenshot tells me I can use this on my phone. The feature depth visible in the screenshots (approval badges, vendor tracking, team roles) suggests a real product, not a weekend project.

But I'm still not at "shut up and take my money." Here's where I'd actually land:

- If the receipt capture is genuinely fast and my contractor actually uses it, I'd upgrade to Home tier. (50% likely — depends entirely on the first-use experience)

## The Three Things That Would Make Me Say "Shut Up and Take My Money"

### 1. Social proof. Any social proof. Please.

I cannot overstate how much this matters. I'm being asked to put my renovation finances — contractor payments, budget details, receipt photos — into a tool made by a company I can't identify, staffed by people I can't find, with no public users I can verify. This is the third review asking for testimonials. The fix is simple: find 3-5 people who've used the product. Get a quote and a first name. Put them on the site. "Caught a $1,800 overcharge from my tile contractor." — Mike R., Denver. That single sentence would do more for conversion than any feature addition.

If the product is too early for real testimonials, own it: "We're in early access. 47 homeowners are tracking $2.3M in renovations. Here's what they're saying." Transparency about being early-stage is more trustworthy than pretending the question doesn't exist.

### 2. Price it like I use it.

I renovate for 3-6 months, then I'm done for 1-3 years. A monthly subscription is designed for software I use every day. A project pass is designed for something I use intensely and then stop.

$49 one-time for a 6-month project pass. I would buy this. Today. Without reading another word on the site. It feels like buying a tool — a thing I need for a specific job. $20/month feels like a subscription — a thing that quietly drains my bank account after the job is done.

Alternatively, let me pause my subscription. "Project done? Freeze your account. Your data stays safe. Reactivate anytime for your next project." This removes the anxiety of paying for something I'm not using.

### 3. Show me the receipt capture in motion.

A 15-second GIF or video: Phone camera opens. Receipt is photographed. Amount is auto-detected. Category is suggested. Budget dashboard updates. Done.

If that flow takes under 10 seconds, I'm switching from my shoebox of receipts. If it takes 2 minutes of manual data entry, I'm staying with my spreadsheet. The site describes this feature but never demonstrates it. This is like selling a car without letting anyone see it drive.

## Bottom Line

Fierro is better than it was in v2. The screenshots are the biggest improvement — they make the product tangible, prove it exists, and show me what I'd actually be using. The composite score moved from 5.9 to 6.3, and every half-point gain was driven by those visuals. The core copy is still strong. The pain point understanding is still excellent. The product concept still fills a real gap.

But the site is still leaving money on the table in two massive ways:

**Trust is still broken.** 3.5/10 on a financial product is not viable for cautious homeowners. Three review cycles with the same #1 recommendation — add testimonials — and still nothing. This is the single highest-impact, lowest-effort fix available. Until real humans vouch for this product on the site, a meaningful percentage of interested visitors will bounce because they can't answer the question "has anyone else actually used this?"

**Pricing still doesn't fit my life.** I don't renovate year-round. A monthly subscription is the wrong shape for my usage pattern. A project pass would convert me instantly. A subscription pause would keep me as a returning customer. Without either, I'll sign up for free, maybe upgrade for one project, cancel, and probably not come back.

The path from 6.3 to 8+ is clear and achievable: add social proof (testimonials + team page), offer homeowner-native pricing (project pass or pause), and show the product in action (receipt capture demo). These aren't moonshot features — they're marketing and pricing decisions that could be implemented in weeks.

The bones of this product are solid. The writing is human. The features are right. Now the site needs to close the deal by answering the two questions every homeowner asks before paying for anything: "Do other people use this?" and "Is this going to be worth it for my one project?" Right now, the answer to both is "I think so, but I'm not sure." That's not enough. Make it obvious.
